Case Study 02 · DC Public Library

DC Public Library
Website Redesign

Re-architected a 12-year-old library website around two unmet user needs — unified search across catalog and site content, and a frictionless path to getting a library card online.

Lead UX Designer

The Problem

DC Library's website had not been substantively redesigned in over a decade. A revamp was underway in 2020 — but it wasn't being built around what users actually needed. Quantitative data pointed clearly to two unresolved user pain points:

  1. Finding relevant information on the website
  2. Getting a library card online

The revamp prioritized neither.

"Customers type in a keyword and expect information from either the website or catalog to show in results. People expect our website search to behave like Google." — Customer-facing library staff

Research Methods

  • Stakeholder Mapping — Mapped internal and external stakeholders to surface competing priorities
  • Quantitative Analytics (VOP) — Analyzed Voice of the Patron survey data alongside site analytics
  • User Interviews — Qualitative interviews with patrons and staff
  • Card Sorting — Validated IA assumptions with real users

Root Cause Analysis

Insight 1 — Catalog Redirect Disorientation Users searching site content were silently redirected to a 3rd-party catalog — a jarring context switch most users didn't recognize or recover from.

Insight 2 — Fragmented Search Systems Catalog search and website search were completely separate. Users had to know which system to use.

Insight 3 — Content Inconsistency at Scale 150 editors publishing weekly created quality variance that buried relevant results.

Design Strategy

The core decision: a single unified search interface presenting catalog items, events, and site content together in an "All Results" view — with tabbed filters to narrow results without understanding back-end system boundaries.

Get a Card Redesign

Streamlined personal details form with a contextual FAQ accordion directly below — answering the exact questions that caused users to abandon mid-form:

  • Who can apply
  • After you complete the form
  • Validating your account & transitioning to permanent card
  • Renewing your card
  • If you do not have a permanent address

Outcome

Both deliverables shipped and remain live on dclibrary.org. Phase 2 scoped to include FRBR-based record grouping, event registration from within search, and expanded sign-in context.
